What Have We Studied?

In past research, we’ve looked at how scientists doing stand-up comedy can influence engagement with and perceptions of science.

Then, we saw this in Vanity Fair…🤔

You can read this article here.

And also this… 🤔

We read an article about this study that said men are funnier than women. But the first author is a man. So we just assumed he was joking the entire time.

We wondered how this might affect scientists doing comedy

👩‍🔬 Specifically, we wondered whether audiences might perceive some scientists as more funny (and, therefore, more warm and benevolent) compared to others.

So…

We conducted a survey in which we embedded an experiment to examine how communicator characteristics (e.g. gender, race, and credentials) might impact how funny people thought they were and whether they were effective at communicating science!

What Did We Do?

Kasha wrote an original 2-min stand-up set of science jokes and recruited four comedians to record it. 😄

Amy, Ashley, Mike, and Sara worked on creating a survey instrument. 📉

We had 8 conditions in our experiment

  1. White male comedian
  2. White female comedian
  3. Black male comedian
  4. Black female comedian
  5. White male scientist
  6. White female scientist
  7. Black male scientist
  8. Black female scientist

Respondents only saw one of the 8 conditions

There were only 4 comedians who recorded the jokes. 📽 And their scripts were identical.

To manipulate their credentials, we included a banner that identified the speaker as a scientist or a comedian.

Then, we asked them how funny they thought the speaker in the video was by asking them to self-report their experience of humor (mirth) and how effective they were at communicating science.

There was little difference in mirth among conditions!

Mean and standard errors of mirth for each experimental condition.

Although mirth was strongly associated with speaker effectiveness, a speaker’s race and gender did not affect speaker effectiveness.

But…

Speaker credentials moderated the influence of mirth on effectiveness

At low levels of mirth, scientists are perceived as less effective communicators.

But at higher levels of mirth, scientists are perceived to be as effective as comedians.

So what?

Humor as the equalizer of perceived communicator effectiveness

Comedians are perceived to be better communicators than scientists among respondents who reported lower levels of mirth. 🙀

Actually, this is not surprising when we consider warmth and the differences between scientists and comedians at connecting with audiences.

But if one can make an audience laugh, then perceptions of communicator effectiveness are higher, regardless of credentials. 😂
